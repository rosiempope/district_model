# Dalkia findings pack — what the model says, organised by decision

Every number below is generated by a reproducible study against the live engine
(212 tests passing; each study writes its own CSVs, figures and findings.md to
`output/`). Billing is always parity-capped: no scheme is ever allowed to
manufacture feasibility by charging customers more than their own alternative.

Reference material for the deck: `python -m analysis.archetype_reference_table`
renders the three density archetypes (Dense/Middle/Scarce) that every study
below tests against — one slide-ready table of building mix, floor area,
connections and connection assumptions per archetype
(`output/archetype_reference_table/AR1-2`), plus a per-building detail CSV.

**One structural fact drives everything else** (cost-decomposition study):
~£13,900 per connection — 5.9p/kWh — of scheme cost does not move with scheme
size, against an Ofgem gas cap of 7.33p. That is the hole every other finding
is trying to fill.

---

## 1. Is it affordable? — No scheme funds itself, and GHNF cannot close the gap

`python -m analysis.ghnf_affordability` · figures `output/ghnf_affordability/AF1-3` 

- Every archetype × stack × customer proposition **fails the 10.5% investor
  test**, with GHNF at 40% and at the ~50% ceiling alike.
- The grant needed for zero NPV is **159–341% of GHNF-eligible CAPEX** — three
  to seven times the legal maximum. This is not a "with a bit more grant" gap.
- The Scarce archetype hits the **4.5p/kWh × 15yr output cap**: its grant
  freezes at £2.85m and *no* headline percentage can raise it. Low-demand
  schemes cannot grant-engineer their way out.
- The waterfall (AF2) shows why: 40 years of parity revenue (£7.5–8.4m PV)
  barely clears OPEX (£5.4m PV), leaving almost nothing against ~£21m CAPEX.
- **Customer proposition matters more than grant rate**: switching the
  comparison from gas parity to heat-pump parity (the legal, Energy-Act-2023
  comparison) raises the affordable tariff from ~10.9p to ~14.6p/kWh — worth
  more than the entire GHNF award on most cases.

**The one lever that reaches zero**: capturing the capital the customer avoids
by not buying their own heat pump. On Birmingham Central (60 GWh, anchor-led),
owner NPV at 10.5% crosses zero between **50% and 75% capture** of avoided
customer capital via connection charges — and the customer is still never worse
off than self-supply (`output/contractor_view/capture_avoided_capital.csv`).

## 2. What is the best energy source? — EfW heat, and the price ceiling is £83/MWh

`python -m analysis.source_frontier` · figures `output/source_frontier/SF1-3`

- **EfW + ASHP + gas peak is the best carbon-compliant stack on every
  archetype** — £9–12m better NPV than plain ASHP + gas on the Dense case.
- **WSHP and GSHP beat ASHP** (stable 11–12 °C source vs winter air) by
  £1–3m at identical sizing; worth pursuing where a river or ground array
  genuinely exists.
- Gas-only still has the least-bad NPV and **always fails the carbon gate** —
  it is the counterfactual, not a candidate. Electric-boiler-only fails both
  carbon (149–204 g) and economics (worst NPV in the set).
- Data-centre heat + booster passes carbon on compact generic cases but fails
  on the sprawling ones and on both real Exeter networks — it only works where
  a large confirmed source displaces more gas-peak running.
- **Negotiation numbers** (vs self-building ASHP): EfW heat is worth up to its
  displacement value, ≈ **£83/MWh** (the ASHP's own marginal heat cost); every
  £10/MWh on the price costs the owner ≈ £1.1m NPV, so the £5–10/MWh range
  quoted for real EfW offtakes leaves the full advantage intact. Maximum
  transmission leg to the plant: ≈ **6.3 km** of extra trunk.

## 3. What linear density is "good"? — Necessary, never sufficient

`python -m analysis.source_frontier` · figure `SF2_density_frontier.png`

- NPV improves with density but **flattens far below zero**: even 29 MWh/m/yr
  (a 450 m route — beyond-best-practice compact) is still −£13.8m.
- Required tariff **never approaches the 7.33p gas cap** at any density tested
  (minimum ≈ 22p). Density fixes the pipe cost; it cannot fix the
  per-connection fixed cost.
- Sprawl has a second, less obvious penalty: network losses backed by gas peak
  push carbon intensity over 100 g on long routes (Scarce ≥ 13 km), **killing
  GHNF eligibility entirely**.
- Screening rule of thumb from the sweep: below ~3 MWh/m/yr the required
  tariff runs away (>40p); above ~15 MWh/m/yr further compaction buys almost
  nothing. Screen on **fixed cost per connection first, density second**.

## 4. Anchors and BUS — anchors win, and BUS widens the gap exactly as suspected

`python -m analysis.anchor_bus_sweep` · figures `output/anchor_bus_sweep/AB1-2`

- Under heat-pump-parity billing, owner NPV improves monotonically with anchor
  share: **−£13.1m fully domestic → −£7.9m at 95% anchors** on a fixed zone.
- **BUS costs the owner up to £2.5m NPV on the domestic-led end and only
  £0.13m at 95% anchors** — the grant cuts domestic customers' alternative by
  £7,500/installation, which parity billing converts directly into lost
  network revenue; anchors above 45 kWth are untouched.
- **Social housing flips it back**: BUS excludes social housing, so
  social-housing-led zones bill as if BUS did not exist — the strongest
  domestic proposition available.
- Under gas parity, anchors actually *hurt* slightly (their per-kWh gas
  alternative is cheaper). The anchor thesis is specifically a
  **heat-pump-era thesis** — which is the era the Energy Act 2023 zoning
  framework legislates for.

## 5. Where should Dalkia sit? — Design + M&E + controls + O&M; civils in someone else's contract

`python -m analysis.dalkia_roles` + `analysis.contractor_view` · figures `DR1-2`, `CV1-2`

| Role | NPV on declared margins | Civils exposure |
|---|---|---|
| R1 Designer only | £0.26m | none |
| R2 M&E/controls contractor | £0.80m | none |
| **R3 M&E + long-term O&M** | **£1.18m** | **none** |
| R4 Whole-scheme D&B incl civils | £1.32m base | margin wiped at **18.6% overrun** |
| R5 Owner/operator (equity) | **−£21.8m** | all of it, for 40 years |

- Real schemes price **9–51% above** the model's own defensible civils curve
  (DESNZ Birmingham vs SEAI fit), so R4's 18.6% cushion dies *inside the
  observed range* — an extra £0.14m of margin over R3 buys a £7.1m exposure.
- A packaged-civils risk premium only protects the owner above the premium's
  own size; below it, self-insuring is cheaper (DR2).
- Dalkia's whole margin is ~5% of the owner's loss: **contractor profitability
  is not why schemes fail, and cutting it would not save one.**
- The margins/scope split are declared assumptions to be replaced with real
  commercial terms — see `output/contractor_view/findings.md`.

## 6. Is four-pipe cooling ever a good idea? — Not as comfort cooling, even with both levers stacked

`python -m analysis.fourpipe_threshold` · figures `FT1_fourpipe_threshold.png`, `FT2_avoided_ac_capex_capture.png`

- Incremental NPV of adding cooling: **−£4.6m to −£8.8m in every mix tested**,
  and it gets *worse* as cooling demand grows — chiller CAPEX, electricity and
  REPEX outrun AC-parity revenue at UK comfort-cooling load factors (~9–11%).
- Even a 75% shared-civils credit moves the answer by only ~£1.3–1.5m: the
  pipes are not the problem, the plant economics are.
- **Capturing the customer's avoided AC purchase closes most of the rest of
  the gap but still doesn't reach zero.** Same connection-charge mechanism as
  the heat-pump capture lever in §1: the customer still gets cooling-bill
  parity and pays up front only what they'd have spent on their own
  chiller/AC anyway. At 100% capture stacked with the 75% civils credit, the
  best mix improves from −£8.8m to **−£1.4m** — real money, but individual-AC
  capex (£800/kW) is itself too modest next to centralised chiller CAPEX +
  REPEX + 40 years of parity-capped revenue to finish the job.
- Honestly out of scope of the current engine (the strongest remaining upside
  case): heat-recovery chillers and ambient-loop networks, and year-round
  process cooling at load factors well above comfort cooling.

## 7. Does climate change move the answer? — Cooling gets worse before it gets better, not the reverse

`python -m analysis.climate_scenario_sweep` · figures `output/climate_scenario_sweep/CS1-3`

- Heating-only NPV barely moves across 2011–2025 baseline → 2050 central →
  2050 high (RCP8.5 + urban heat island): **−£0.9m to −£1.4m worse** by 2050
  high, across all three archetypes. Gas-parity revenue and CAPEX are both
  climate-insensitive; only demand volume shifts.
- **A warmer climate makes 4-pipe cooling LESS attractive, not more**:
  incremental cooling NPV worsens by **£1.1–2.1m** from baseline to 2050
  high, on every archetype. Cooling demand grows faster than heating demand
  shrinks (annual cooling roughly triples on the Dense archetype by 2050
  high), and AC-parity revenue is capped at what the customer would pay a
  self-supplied unit — so more cooling delivered under a hard price ceiling
  is a bigger loss, not a bigger opportunity.
- **Credibility note for the room, worth having ready if asked**: building
  this comparison surfaced and fixed a real defect in the cooling demand
  model — a peak-sizing term was double-counting climate warming (once via
  the annual CDD-driven share, again via an hours-crossing-threshold ratio on
  the peak), which inflated 2050-scenario cooling peaks by up to ~11x and
  crashed network sizing outright on a full archetype. Fixed, regression-
  tested against real weather data, and the Ealing validation still
  reconciles to 13/13 at ~0% variance (the fix is a no-op at baseline
  climate, which is what Ealing validates against) — see
  `profiles/demand_synthesis.py`'s `_cooling_profile()` docstring.

## 8. The stakeholder story — who makes money on a scheme that loses money

`python -m analysis.contractor_view` · figure `CV1_four_positions.png`

Contractor (3-year exposure, paid from CAPEX) ≈ +£0.8m; operator (fee margin)
≈ +£0.3m; owner (40-year residual) ≈ −£21.8m; GHNF (no cash return) −£3.5m.
The contractors' margins are already *inside* the CAPEX/OPEX that sink the
owner. That asymmetry — 3 years of exposure vs 40 — is why a civils contractor
will happily build a scheme no investor should own.

## The recommendation shape this supports

**Pursue**: anchor-led compact zones · EfW/rivers/ground where confirmed within
~6 km · design + M&E + controls + long-term O&M · HP-parity (zoning-era)
customer propositions · connection charges that capture 50–75% of avoided
customer capital · GHNF at 40% assumed, never 50%.

**Avoid**: owning gas-parity schemes at 10.5% · dispersed domestic-led zones
(BUS actively works against them) · whole-scheme fixed-price civils without
survey-quality route data · default four-pipe cooling — even generously
levered with avoided-AC capture, and even less so on a warming climate ·
speculative data-centre heat without guaranteed capacity and availability.

## Known limitations that bound these claims

The engine is a screening tool, not a bankable model — see MODEL_SUMMARY §12.
Specifically relevant here: BUS's 70 kWth cascade / 300 kWth shared-loop
routes and EPC gating are not modelled (direction: slightly generous to the
individual-HP case); the four-pipe study cannot see heat-recovery chillers;
margins and scope splits in §5 are declared assumptions; connection-charge
capture assumes customers accept paying their avoided capital, which is a
commercial proposition to be tested, not a model output.
